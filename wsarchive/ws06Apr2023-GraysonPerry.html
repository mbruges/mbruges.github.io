Your gaudy evocation can, somehow,

and yet educe a sense of peace, of calm -
the screech of tyres and the nervous squeals
of girls, too young to quite appreciate
the peril they are in, are heard, but these wheels
will not lose traction, skid and flip, no harm
befall these children. They will stay out late

forever, pumped on youth and ecstasy,
on alloy, bass and arrogance, and speed
the back lanes, the urban gyratory,
the wide motorways, never having need
to race back home, for work next day, to bed.
Each girl is buff, each geezer toned and strong,

in crude games of chlamydia roulette.

Now see who comes to line the sparse grass verge,
to toast them in Buckfast and Diamond White:
rat-boys and corn-rowed cheerleaders who urge
them on to pull more burn-outs or to write
their donut Os, as signature, upon
the bleached tarmac of dead suburban streets.
There dogs set up a row and curtains twitch
as pensioners and parents telephone
the cops to plead for quiet, sue for peace -
tranquility, though, is for the rich.

And so, millennia hence, you garish crock,
when all context is lost, galleries razed
to level dust and we're long in the box,
will future poets look on you amazed,
speculate how children might have lived when
you were fired, lives so free and bountiful
and there, beneath a sun a little colder,

who knew the truth was all negotiable
and beauty in the gift of the beholder..

Comprehension

Answer the following questions about the poem:

  1. What reference is made in the title of the poem, and why? (You should click here before answering)
  2. What kind of object is being described in the poem?
  3. Who is the creator of this object, according to the title?
  4. What scenes and images are depicted on the urn?
  5. What atmosphere is created by these scenes, according to the poem? How is this unexpected?
  6. What does the final stanza suggest about how future generations might interpret the urn?

Language analysis

Discuss the poetic techniques used in the poem and their effects:

  • The use of enjambment and caesura in conveying a sense of speed and chaos
  • The mixture of poetic and colloquial language to evoke both the artistry of the urn and the contemporary scenes it depicts
  • The rhyme scheme in giving the poem a rhythmic, fluid quality reflecting the motion of the scenes
  • Sensory imagery in bringing the urn and its scenes to life

Discussion

The poem suggests art can give beauty and meaning even to unconventional subjects. Discuss the ways in which the poet conveys this theme in relation to the Grayson Perry urn and its depictions of youth culture.

You could consider:

  • How the chaotic, gritty subject matter of the scenes is transformed through Perry's artistic style
  • The sense of energy, freedom and youthful joy the poem attributes to the urn's depictions
  • The timeless, universal quality suggested in the final stanza
  • The juxtaposition of the crude and chaotic with the artistic and harmonious
  • What is the relationship between youth culture and society as depicted in the poem?
  • What is the role of art in capturing and reflecting contemporary culture?
